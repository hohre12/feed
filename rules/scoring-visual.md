# Visual Content Self-Scoring Rubric (Carousel / Card News)

Score your own generated carousel content strictly using these criteria.
**Be harsh.** The moment you go easy on your own design spec, quality drops.

This rubric applies **only to visual/format quality** of Instagram carousels. It is used alongside the base `scoring.md` rubric, which evaluates text quality (caption copy and slide copy). Both scorecards must pass independently for the post to be published.

---

## Dual Scoring System

Instagram carousel posts receive two separate evaluations:

**A. Text Quality** — evaluated by `scoring.md` (5 dimensions: twist, relatability, humor, brevity, human-feel)
- Applied to caption text and all slide copy
- `brevity` is reinterpreted as **text density per slide** (not overall post length)
- `twist` and `humor` may be marked N/A for informational carousels (e.g., `curated-list` style applies fixed scores per `scoring.md` style-specific override rules)

**B. Visual / Format Quality** — evaluated by this file (5 dimensions below)
- Applied to carousel structure, flow, readability, and design consistency
- Scored independently from text quality

**Final verdict requires both averages to be 7.0 or above.** If either fails, the entire post fails.

---

## Scoring Dimensions

| Dimension | Key | What It Measures |
|-----------|-----|------------------|
| Readability | `readability` | Can text be read comfortably on a 375px-wide phone screen without zooming? Is each line ~30 characters or fewer? Is the slide limited to 5-7 lines? Is font size appropriate throughout? |
| Slide Flow | `slide-flow` | Do slides progress logically? Is the Cover → Body → CTA arc natural? Can each individual slide be understood in isolation? |
| Swipe Hook | `swipe-hook` | Is there a reason to swipe at the end of each slide? Are visual/text hooks present — numbering (e.g., 3/7), incomplete sentences, "continued on next slide" cues? |
| Save Trigger | `save-trigger` | Is this carousel worth bookmarking? Does it contain practical information or reference value the reader will want to return to later? |
| Visual Consistency | `visual-consistency` | Are colors, fonts, spacing, and layout consistent across all slides? Is the brand header/footer identical on every slide? Do content slides include sourced images (screenshots, logos, product visuals)? Text-only slides without images automatically cap this score at 5. |

---

## Score Definitions (Apply Strictly)

### Readability (`readability`)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1-3 | Text is too dense or font is too small to read without zooming. Key information is buried in walls of text. Slide layout actively fights comprehension. |
| 4-6 | Readable, but uncomfortable. Some slides have excessive text. The reader has to work harder than they should. At least one slide causes hesitation. |
| 7-8 | Reads cleanly. Font size, line spacing, and text volume are appropriate. No single slide feels overloaded. Eyes move naturally through the content. |
| 9-10 | Perfect readability. Core message is visible at a glance. Visual hierarchy guides the eye without effort. Each slide could be a standalone poster. |

**Note:** A score of 9-10 requires that even the most text-heavy slide in the carousel satisfies the ~30 char/line, 5-7 line guideline. One overloaded slide cannot average out.

### Slide Flow (`slide-flow`)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1-3 | Slides appear in random order, or the same point is repeated across multiple slides. There is no identifiable progression — the reader cannot tell what comes next or why. |
| 4-6 | There is a discernible flow, but at least one slide feels out of place or redundant. The Cover or CTA may be missing. Transitions feel forced or abrupt. |
| 7-8 | Natural progression. Cover sets up a clear expectation, body delivers on it, CTA closes cleanly. Each slide earns its place. |
| 9-10 | Storytelling level. Every slide not only follows logically from the previous but creates anticipation for the next. The arc feels inevitable in retrospect. |

### Swipe Hook (`swipe-hook`)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1-3 | No reason to swipe after the first slide. The cover delivers everything, or nothing creates curiosity. The carousel could be a single image. |
| 4-6 | Swipe motivation exists on some slides but disappears in the middle. The momentum created by the cover is not sustained. |
| 7-8 | Most slides end with a clear pull toward the next. Numbering, incomplete thoughts, or visual continuation techniques are present and functional. |
| 9-10 | Cannot stop swiping until the last slide. Every transition has a reason — unresolved tension, a cliffhanger, a "wait, but..." moment. |

### Save Trigger (`save-trigger`)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1-3 | No reason to save. The content is either obvious, ephemeral, or too abstract to reference again. One-time consumption only. |
| 4-6 | Mildly useful but not save-worthy. "I'll remember this without saving it" or "I'll just look it up later" reaction. |
| 7-8 | "I should save this for later" reaction. Contains a list, checklist, framework, or insight the reader will want to revisit. The save button is actually pressed. |
| 9-10 | Saved and shared. The carousel is a reference document — the kind of thing forwarded in group chats with "this is actually helpful." |

**Note:** Save Trigger is not about virality. A carousel that is highly shareable but has no re-reference value should not score above 7. A carousel that is niche but genuinely useful to its target audience can score 9-10.

### Visual Consistency (`visual-consistency`)

| Score | Criteria |
|-------|----------|
| 1-3 | Each slide feels designed separately. Colors, fonts, or layouts shift between slides. No sourced images at all — text-only slides on a visual platform. |
| 4-5 | Some slides have images but others are text-only. Inconsistent use of visuals. **Any content slide without a sourced image (screenshot, logo, or product visual) caps this score at 5.** |
| 6 | All content slides have images but visual treatment is inconsistent (different sizes, placements, or overlay styles). |
| 7-8 | All slides follow the same design system with sourced images consistently placed. Brand header and footer are identical. The carousel feels like a single cohesive piece. |
| 9-10 | Professional design level. Every element — spacing, hierarchy, color usage, image treatment, iconography — is unified. Sourced images enhance rather than distract. |

## Passing Criteria

### Hard Floors (below this = mandatory regeneration regardless of average)
- **`readability` below 7** → mandatory regeneration. A carousel that cannot be read on a phone screen has no value.
- **`visual-consistency` below 7** → mandatory regeneration. Brand inconsistency destroys credibility.

### Quality Thresholds
- All 5 dimensions must score 7 or above to pass
- Average 7.0-7.4 → PASS but **auto-improve once** (format is acceptable but not strong enough)
- Average 7.5-7.9 → PASS, publishable
- Average 8.0+ → EXCELLENT, confidently publish

### Regeneration Rules
- Maximum 3 total attempts (1 initial + 2 regenerations)
- When regenerating, explicitly target the low-scoring dimensions
- If still failing after 2 regenerations, select the highest-scoring version and mark it as having failed the quality target

## Self-Scoring Protocol

### Attitude
- **Score strictly.** Do not be generous because layout specification looks correct on paper.
- **Target average is 8.0+.** "Technically acceptable" is not the goal — a carousel that actually gets saved is.
- **7 means "minimum pass."** 8 means "good." 9 means "save-and-share potential."
- If you're averaging below 7.5, the carousel spec needs improvement, not lenient scoring.

### Anti-Inflation Rule (CRITICAL)

LLMs consistently self-score visual specs 0.3-0.5 points higher than honest evaluation because layout correctness is conflated with quality. To counter this:

1. **After scoring, subtract 0.5 from your initial instinct for `readability` and `visual-consistency`.** These two dimensions are where LLMs are most generous — a spec that follows rules gets inflated credit even when the output would still feel crowded or inconsistent in practice. If you initially think "readability 8" → write 7.5. If you initially think "visual-consistency 8" → write 7.5.
2. **The "would I actually save this?" test**: Before finalizing scores, ask honestly: "If this carousel appeared on my Instagram feed right now, would I press the save button without hesitation?" If there is any hesitation, `save-trigger` cannot exceed 7.5.
3. **Uniformity inflation check**: If all slides follow identical text length and identical layout structure (e.g., every slide has exactly one heading + two bullet points), cap `visual-consistency` at 7 maximum. Real carousels designed by humans have natural variation — some slides are text-heavy, some are image-dominant, some break the pattern intentionally.
4. **Swipe hook pattern check**: If the only swipe hook is the slide number (e.g., "3/7"), cap `swipe-hook` at 7. Numbering alone is necessary but not sufficient for a strong hook.

### Verification Questions (Mandatory Before Scoring — Do Not Skip)

Before scoring, answer these 5 questions **honestly.** The answers set the ceiling for your scores.

1. **If you saw this carousel's cover slide on your feed, would you swipe to the next slide?**
   -> "I'd probably scroll past" -> `swipe-hook` capped at 6, overall average capped at 7
2. **Would you save this carousel?**
   -> "No" -> `save-trigger` capped at 6
3. **Can you read every slide without zooming on a 375px screen?**
   -> "Not all of them" -> `readability` capped at 6
4. **Do all slides look like they belong to the same carousel?**
   -> "Some feel off" -> `visual-consistency` capped at 6
5. **Does this carousel look like it was generated by an AI tool?**
   -> "Even a little bit, yes" -> overall average capped at 7.5

### Safe Carousel Deduction

"A carousel that is visually clean, inoffensive, and technically correct, but nobody would save or share it" is a safe carousel.

**Detection criteria** — the carousel is "safe" if 2 or more of the following are true:
- The topic and angle are obvious (e.g., "운동의 중요성", "독서를 해야 하는 이유" — generic life advice)
- Every slide follows the exact same structural template with no variation
- The CTA asks for engagement but gives the reader no specific reason to act
- The content could apply to any account in any niche — it has no point of view
- Nobody would tag a friend to share it

**Implementation rules for safe-carousel deduction:**
1. Run the 5 detection criteria after initial scoring
2. If 2+ criteria are met, apply the following deductions:
   - `save-trigger` -1 point (generic information has no reference value)
   - `swipe-hook` -1 point (no point of view means no reason to keep reading)
3. If the overall average is 7.5 or above after deductions, **mandatory re-review** — a safe carousel should not score this high
4. Log which detection criteria triggered so the regeneration can specifically address them

## Style-Specific Weight Overrides

Scoring emphasis shifts depending on the carousel style. These overrides do not change the pass threshold — all dimensions still require 7 — but they define which dimension is the **primary quality signal** during regeneration targeting.

| Style | Primary Dimension | Rationale |
|-------|-------------------|-----------|
| `curated-list` | `save-trigger` | The entire purpose of a list carousel is reference value. If it isn't save-worthy, it has failed its core function. `swipe-hook` is partially automated by numbering (3/7 pattern), so it is treated as secondary. |
| `insight` | `slide-flow` | Educational carousels live or die on logical progression. A reader who loses the thread of the argument will not finish the carousel or retain the insight. |
| `debate` | `swipe-hook` | The reader must be genuinely curious about the opposing argument to swipe. If both sides are telegraphed on the cover, there is no reason to continue. |

## Combined Final Verdict

At the end of carousel generation, output **both** scorecards consecutively:

1. Text quality scorecard (from `scoring.md`) — applied to caption and slide copy
2. Visual quality scorecard (this rubric) — applied to carousel structure and design

**Final PASS requires:**
- Text average ≥ 7.0
- Visual average ≥ 7.0
- No individual dimension below 7 in either scorecard
- No hard floor violations (`readability` and `visual-consistency` must both be ≥ 7)

If either scorecard fails, the overall verdict is FAIL even if the other scorecard is EXCELLENT.

---

## Output Format

Display the visual scorecard immediately after the text scorecard. Format:

```
---
| Dimension | Score | Reasoning |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| Readability (readability) | 8 | All slides under 30 chars/line, font size appropriate throughout |
| Slide Flow (slide-flow) | 7 | Flow is natural but slide 3 feels tangential to the main arc |
| Swipe Hook (swipe-hook) | 8 | Numbering (3/7) + incomplete sentence on slide 4 sustains momentum |
| Save Trigger (save-trigger) | 9 | Practical checklist format — reader will want to reference this again |
| Visual Consistency (visual-consistency) | 8 | Dark theme + blue accent maintained across all slides |
| **Average** | **8.0** | |
| **Verdict** | **EXCELLENT** | |
---
```

**The Reasoning column is mandatory.** Do not just write a number. Explain in one line why that score was given, referencing a specific slide or element where possible.

If the verdict is FAIL, specify:
1. Which dimensions fell short
2. Which hard floor was violated (if any)
3. What must be specifically improved in the regeneration (not generic advice — name the slide or element)
